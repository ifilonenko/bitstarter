The mitochondrion is a membrane-enclosed structure found in most eukaryotic cells. The mitochondria are coined as the cellular power plants because they generate most of the cell's supply of ATP, which is used as a source of chemical energy. In addition to supplying cellular energy, mitochondria are involved in other tasks such as signaling, cellular differentiation, cell death, as well as the control of the cell cycle and cell growth. They have also played a huge role in several human diseases including mitochondrial disorders, cardiac dysfunction, and aging. Mitochondrias have also been used in stem cell research as promising cures.

Due to the fact that the mitochondria ages with us, a synthetic mechanized system that produces ATP could replace the dying mitochondria cell. More Info on Electron Transport Chain

This research would be focused on the Electron Transport chain and the 4 Complexes that govern the ETC. Each of them have a different purpose, and a proper modeling of each would enable us to understand the proton and electron movement of the entire ETC and in turn the mitochondria.

Complex 1
In Complex I two electrons are removed from NADH and transferred to a lipid-soluble carrier, ubiquinone (Q). The reduced product, ubiquinol (QH2) freely diffuses within the membrane, and Complex 1 translocates four protons (H+) across the membrane, thus producing a proton gradient. Complex I is one of the main sites at which premature electron leakage to oxygen occurs, thus being one of the main sites of production of harmful superoxide.During this process, four protons are translocated from the mitochondrial matrix to the intermembrane space.
Complex 2
In Complex 2 additional electrons are delivered into the quinone pool (Q) originating from succinate and transferred (via FAD) to Q. Complex 2 consists of four protein subunits: SDHA, SDHB, SDHC, and SDHD. Other electron donors (e.g., fatty acids an glycerol 3-phosphate) also direct electrons into Q (via FAD).
Complex 3
In Complex 3, the Q-cycle contributes to the proton gradient by an asymmetric absorption/release of protons. Two electrons are removed from QH2 at the QO site and sequentially transferred to two molecules of cytochrome c, a water-soluble electron carrier located within the intermembrane space. The two other electrons sequentially pass across the protein to the Qi site where the quinone part of ubiquinone is reduced to quinol. A proton gradient is formed by two quinol oxidations at the Qo site to form one quinol at the Qi site. In total six protons are translocated: two protons reduce quinone to quinol and four protons are released from two ubiquinol molecules.When electron transfer is reduced by a high membrane potential or respiratory inhibitors such as antimycin A, Complex 3 may leak electrons to molecular oxygen, resulting in superoxide formation.
Complex 4
In Complex 4 sometimes called cytochrome A3, four electrons are removed from four molecules of cytochrome c and transferred to molecular oxygen (O2), producing two molecules of water. At the same time, four protons are removed from the mitochondrial matrix (although only two are translocated across the membrane), contributing to the proton gradient. The activity of cytochrome c oxidase is inhibited by cyanide.
